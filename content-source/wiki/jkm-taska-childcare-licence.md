# JKM TASKA Licence (childcare centres — children under 4)

**Status:** established
**Last updated:** 2026-06-22
**Sources:** `RAW/JKM License/TASKA/Panduan Permohonan Pendaftaran TASKA [FINAL].pdf`, `RAW/JKM License/TASKA/2) GARIS-PANDUAN-PENUBUHAN-TADIKA-DAN-TASKA.pdf`, `RAW/JKM License/TASKA/1) AKTA TAMAN ASUHAN KANAK-KANAK.pdf`, `RAW/JKM License/TASKA/3) Borang Baru eJKM.pdf`, `RAW/JKM License/TASKA/General Process.docx`, `RAW/JKM License/Daycare Centre [JKM]/Flow Process for Opening a Daycare Center.docx`

## Summary

A TASKA (Taman Asuhan Kanak-Kanak) is a childcare centre that takes in four or more children under the age of four from more than one household, for a fee. Any such premises must register with the Department of Social Welfare (Jabatan Kebajikan Masyarakat, JKM) under the Child Care Centre Act 1984 [Act 308] and the Child Care Centre Regulations 2012 before it operates — running an unregistered TASKA is an offence under section 6 of the Act. Registration is made in person at the District Social Welfare Office (Pejabat Kebajikan Masyarakat Daerah, PKMD) for the area where the centre will operate, using Borang A. JKM processes a complete application within 21 working days, and the certificate is valid for five years. The scheme sits beside, but is separate from, the MOE kindergarten (tadika) licence for children aged four and above, and the JKM Pusat Jagaan licence for older children, the disabled, the elderly, and women.

## Body

### What a TASKA is and who must register

Section 2 of Act 308 defines a TASKA as any premises that admits four or more children under the age of four, from more than one household, to be cared for in return for payment. Section 4 requires every such centre to be registered, and section 6 makes it an offence to run or take part in managing an unregistered TASKA. The operator may be a private business or a non-governmental organisation (NGO). The governing instruments are Act 308 itself, the Child Care Centre Regulations 2012, the compounding-of-offences regulations 2011, and the Director General's circulars — including circular 1 of 2015, which mandates CCTV in registered TASKAs.

### The four types of TASKA

Section 5 of the Act registers a TASKA under one of four types, and the type sets the floor-space rule and the registration fee:

- **TASKA di Rumah (home-based)** — admits 4 to 9 children, run in the operator's own residence.
- **TASKA Institusi (institutional)** — admits 10 or more children; set up by a private operator or NGO.
- **TASKA di Tempat Kerja (workplace)** — admits 10 or more children; set up by an employer for the welfare of staff.
- **TASKA Komuniti (community)** — admits 10 or more children in a lower-income area (household income under RM2,000 urban / under RM1,200 rural) and receives federal or state assistance.

### Who can be the operator

The operator must be a Malaysian citizen aged 18 or above who has passed the recognised childcare training course — either the Basic Child Care Course (Kursus Asas Asuhan Kanak-Kanak, KAAK) or the PERMATA Early Childhood Care and Education course (KAP). For a company, at least one director must meet that test; for a partnership, at least one partner; for a society, at least one office-bearer. The operator, manager, and supervisor must all hold the KAAK/KAP certificate. Caregivers (pengasuh) must be Malaysian citizens aged 18 or above and must pass the KAAK course within 12 months of starting work. The application form (Borang A) records caregivers as female only.

### Caregiver-to-child and floor-space ratios

JKM sets a minimum caregiver-to-child ratio by age, with a tighter ratio for children with disabilities (OKU):

| Age | Normal children | OKU children |
|-----|-----------------|--------------|
| Birth to 1 year | 1 : 3 | 2 : 3 |
| Over 1 to 3 years | 1 : 5 | 2 : 5 |
| Over 3 to 4 years | 1 : 10 | 3 : 10 |

Floor space per child is 2.5 square metres for a TASKA di Rumah and 3.5 square metres for an institutional, workplace, or community TASKA (the 3.5 figure includes shared spaces such as kitchen, store, and toilet).

### Minimum standards and technical-agency support

Beyond the operator, ratio, and space rules, the centre must register the business with SSM (or the Registrar of Societies for an NGO), display a balanced food menu and an age-appropriate daily activity schedule, hold suitable and sufficient furniture and equipment, keep the prescribed records on site, and maintain cleanliness and safety inside and outside the premises. Before JKM will register, the applicant must obtain support or approval from the technical agencies: the local authority (PBT), the Fire and Rescue Department (JBPM), the health department (Jabatan Kesihatan), and — in Sarawak only — the Land and Survey Department.

The fire requirements include emergency "EXIT" lighting and emergency lights on dual power supply (of a JBPM-approved type) and 2 × 6kg dry-powder extinguishers. The health requirements cover staff medical clearance (TB, HIV, communicable disease, mental health) and typhoid vaccination, a food-handler course, adequate lighting (over 200 lux) and natural ventilation (window opening over 10% of floor area), a no-smoking sign at the gate, no operation inside a factory building, an isolation area for communicable-disease cases, safe toilets and kitchen, a first-aid kit, and HFMD disinfection procedures. The health checklist is also used to monitor the centre every six months.

### How registration works

The process runs through the PKMD for the area where the centre will operate:

1. **Pre-registration.** Consult the authorised TASKA officer (Pegawai Diberi Kuasa, PDBK) at the nearest PKMD, collect Borang A (First Schedule, Regulation 4, Child Care Centre Regulations 2012) and the application checklist, register the business with SSM or ROS, and apply to the technical agencies for their support.
2. **New application.** Submit the completed Borang A with the full set of supporting documents to the PDBK at the PKMD.
3. **Inspection.** The PDBK inspects the premises.
4. **Fee.** Pay the registration fee once the application is checked and found complete (see below).
5. **Approval.** The PDBK processes the application; the PKMD officer endorses it if the JKM minimum standards are met; a complete application goes to the State Director of Social Welfare for approval.
6. **Certificate.** If the state office raises no query, the centre is registered and a Certificate of Registration is issued by the State Director, valid for five years. Any correction to the certificate must be notified in writing within 14 days.

Renewal follows the same procedure as a new application and should be started three months before the certificate expires. After registration, the PDBK monitors the centre at any time for ongoing compliance.

### Fees

The JKM registration guide states the registration fee as RM50 for a TASKA di Rumah and RM250 for institutional, workplace, and community TASKAs, payable by postal order, bank draft, or cash. The town-planning establishment guideline breaks the institutional figure down as RM300 for a new application (RM50 process fee + RM250 certificate) and RM290 on renewal (RM30 process + RM250 certificate). Read together, the RM250 is the certificate component and the process fee is added on top; quote the RM300 new-application total for an institutional TASKA. These JKM fees are separate from the planning-permission, building-plan, and business-premises licence fees the local authority charges where a change of use or new building is involved.

### The premises and planning dimension

The town-planning establishment guideline (issued by JPBD, the Federal Department of Town and Country Planning) treats TASKA and tadika together on the premises side. It sets four premises categories (special sites with or without a building already erected, and conversion of existing buildings) and, where a change of use or new construction is needed, routes the project through planning permission (Kebenaran Merancang) and building-plan approval before the local-authority business-premises licence is issued. For a straightforward home-based or existing-unit TASKA this planning step may not arise; for a purpose-built or converted centre it does, and it carries its own fees and timeline — much like the commercial-to-education premises conversion that arises for MOE centres.

### Practical sequencing and premises (team notes)

MISHU's working process flow sharpens the official steps with the order that actually works on the ground:

- **Settle the premises with PBT first.** Before committing, confirm with the local authority's building/planning unit whether the location will be approved. If it will, PBT changes the premises use (tukar guna) to "TASKA" — for example, converting a corner-lot residence. Only then rent or buy.
- **Engage a TASKA-familiar architect** for the renovation; they advise on the required safety facilities (emergency exits, fire equipment, OKU access). If the centre will admit a child with disabilities (OKU), build in OKU facilities — an OKU toilet, ramps, and so on — from the start.
- **Apply once renovation is about 80% complete** to PBT and Bomba; the JKM application can run in parallel rather than waiting.
- **Realistic timelines:** PBT and MOH take roughly two months; JKM takes the longest — expect six months or more. Plan the client's expectations around the JKM leg, not the council leg.
- **Premises quota by zone:** in a commercial area there is no quota, so a centre can open freely; in a residential area, check the availability/quota with the local council first, as residential approvals are capped.
- **Ownership:** the centre can be an Sdn Bhd with at least one local shareholder (foreigners are allowed as co-shareholders) or a sole proprietorship — but remember the operator/director test still requires a Malaysian citizen with the KAAK/KAP certificate, so foreign ownership does not remove the local-operator requirement. Note that a "daycare" taking older children runs to ages 0–12, beyond the TASKA 0–4 band, and is treated differently.
