# WRT & USS Licences (foreign-owned trade and services)

**Status:** established
**Last updated:** 2026-06-20
**Sources:** `RAW/Wholesale, Retail & Trading (WRT) License/CHECKLIST/WRT_Guideline.pdf`, `RAW/Wholesale, Retail & Trading (WRT) License/CHECKLIST/senarai_semak_permohonan_baru_dan_pembaharuan_menjalankan_perdagangan_pengedaran_borang__runcit_wrt-converted.pdf`, `RAW/Wholesale, Retail & Trading (WRT) License/FORMS/FORMAT BORANG USS (1).doc`, `RAW/Wholesale, Retail & Trading (WRT) License/QUOTATION/WRT License.docx`, `RAW/Wholesale, Retail & Trading (WRT) License/uss-wrt-web-digest.md`

## Summary

WRT and USS are sibling licences from the same ministry (KPDN, formerly MDTCC), both for companies with foreign (non-Malaysian) shareholding. WRT — Wholesale, Retail & Trade — covers foreign-owned businesses trading in goods. USS — Unregulated Services Sector — covers foreign-owned businesses providing services that aren't otherwise regulated. The rule of thumb: if a foreign-owned company sells goods it needs WRT; if it provides services it needs USS; if it does both, it needs one licence chosen by where most of its revenue comes from. Because the two cover the field between them, **whenever a consultant scopes a business type for a client with any foreign shareholding, check both** — applying for the wrong one means rejection and a restart of the two-to-three-month process. For MISHU this sits close to our immigration work: a foreign-owned company generally needs its WRT or USS approval in place before it can apply for Employment Passes for expatriate staff. The pre-screening job is to confirm whether the activity is goods (WRT) or services (USS), check the minimum paid-up capital, and flag activities that foreigners simply aren't allowed to do.

## Body

### WRT or USS? Check both first

Both licences apply only to companies with foreign shareholding, and between them they cover almost any foreign-owned business. Decide which one before anything else:

- **WRT** — the company trades in goods (wholesale, retail, distribution, import/export).
- **USS** — the company provides services that aren't separately regulated (see the USS section below for the service categories).
- **Both activities** — only one licence is needed, chosen by where the majority of revenue comes from.

The two share the same ministry (KPDN), the same WRT 1 form, the same document checklist, the same BLESS submission, the same two-to-three-month processing time, and the same one-to-three-year validity. The practical risk is picking the wrong one: KPDN rejects a misfiled application, and the client restarts the whole process. USS is the less well-known of the pair, so it's the one that gets missed. When a consultant is asked about a business type for a foreign-shareholding client, run both past the activity before scoping.

### Who needs it and who grants it

The licence applies to foreign business operators engaged in distributive trade. Under the guideline, "foreign participation" means a non-citizen individual (including permanent residents), a foreign company or institution, or a local company in which foreigners hold more than 50% of the voting rights. With effect from 6 January 2010, all proposals for foreign involvement in distributive trade need KPDN/MDTCC approval — this covers acquiring an interest, mergers and takeovers, opening, relocating, or expanding branches and outlets, and buying property to run distributive trade.

KPDN is the secretariat to the Committee on Distributive Trade, which is the reference point for all applications. A purely Malaysian-owned company does not need a WRT licence — this is a foreign-equity requirement.

### What counts as distributive trade

Distributive trade is the chain of activities that channel goods and services to intermediaries for resale or to final buyers. It captures wholesalers, retailers, franchisors and franchisees, direct sellers, suppliers, and commission agents or representatives of international trading companies.

- **Wholesale trade** — sale or resale of goods and services to other distributors buying for their business.
- **Retail trade** — sale or resale of goods and services to the general public for final consumption.

It does not include manufacturing companies, or companies granted MIDA regional-establishment status (International Procurement Centres, Regional Distribution Centres, Operational Headquarters). Products governed by other Acts — petroleum, pharmaceuticals, toxic substances, explosives, arms, agricultural raw materials, live animals — sit under their own regimes.

### Business formats and minimum capital (the pre-screening table)

Minimum capital here means shareholders' funds (paid-up capital plus reserves). The format determines the threshold, so this is the first thing to check with a foreign-investor client:

- **Hypermarket** — sales floor 5,000 sqm or more; minimum RM50 million; must reserve at least 30% equity for Bumiputera; standalone, on the outskirts of major towns; one allowed per 250,000 residents; not within a 3.5 km radius of residential areas or town centres.
- **Superstore** — 3,000 to under 4,999 sqm; minimum RM25 million; only companies already operating a hypermarket may apply; one per 200,000 residents.
- **Departmental store** — minimum RM20 million (reviewed every 3 years); may include a supermarket of not more than 2,000 sqm.
- **Specialty store** — one main brand or product line (furniture, electrical, healthcare, optical, footwear, apparel, sports, books, jewellery, electronics, pharmacy, and similar); minimum RM1 million per outlet.
- **Various other distribution formats** — considered case by case on socio-economic merit; minimum RM1 million per outlet.
- **Franchisor / franchisee** — governed by the Franchise Act 1998; minimum capital assessed case by case.

All foreign-equity distributive trade companies must be incorporated locally under the Companies Act. A foreign branch has not been allowed to carry on wholesale or retail trade in Malaysia since 1 November 1995.

### Standing conditions for foreign-equity companies

Every distributive trade company with foreign equity must: appoint Bumiputera director(s); build a workforce reflecting Malaysia's racial composition at all levels including management; have clear plans to assist Bumiputera participation; hire at least 1% of the hypermarket workforce from persons with disabilities; use local airports and ports for import and export; use local legal and professional services; submit annual financial reports to KPDN; and comply with local authority by-laws. Larger-format stores also carry a commitment to allocate at least 30% of shelf space (SKUs) to Bumiputera SME products within three years.

### What foreigners are not allowed to do

The guideline closes several activities to foreign involvement entirely. Flag these early — they are not licensable for a foreign-owned client:

- Supermarket or mini-market under 3,000 sqm; provision shops and general vendors; 24-hour convenience stores; news agents; medical halls; fuel stations (with or without a convenience store); permanent wet-market and pavement stores; textiles; non-exclusive restaurants; bistros; and jewellery shops.

Note the restaurant rule in particular: the Ministry only approves an **exclusive** restaurant for foreign equity. To qualify as exclusive, the restaurant must be at least one shop lot (1,000–1,500 sq ft), be air-conditioned, have a unique and specialised menu, have decor in line with that menu, and meet cleanliness and location standards. An ordinary restaurant cannot be foreign-owned via WRT.

### USS — the services-sector sibling

USS (Unregulated Services Sector) is what a foreign-owned company applies for when its business is providing services rather than trading goods. It is issued by the same ministry (KPDN) and exists for the same reason as WRT: to manage foreign participation while protecting local businesses. A foreign-owned company that doesn't fall under WRT will usually fall under USS instead — so the two should always be checked together.

Service categories that typically need USS:

- Transport and logistics.
- Real estate and property management.
- Equipment leasing and maintenance.
- Professional, business, and research services (consultancies, training, technical services, and similar).
- Community and personal care services.

(KPDN publishes an official list of USS-covered services; obtain and ingest it for the definitive scope before advising on a borderline case.)

General requirements for USS mirror WRT closely: the company must be incorporated under the Companies Act 2016, hold a minimum paid-up capital of RM1 million, and already have its local council (PBT) premises and signboard approvals in place. The application uses the same WRT 1 form, the same document checklist, and the same BLESS submission described below.

The official USS application form confirms the framework: titled "Application Form for the Establishment of Distributive Trade in Malaysia — Unregulated Services Sector," it sits under the same distributive-trade regime as WRT and collects the same picture of the company — establishment details and MyCoID, the USS trade type, headquarters or branch, sales floor area, parent company, share capital (authorised and issued/paid-up), the equity structure split across Bumiputera, non-Bumiputera, and foreign holders, fixed assets, investment, and the employment structure. The service-category list above still rests on secondary sources, so confirm a borderline activity against KPDN's official USS service list before advising.

### Exemption: make the company majority Malaysian

Because both WRT and USS apply only to foreign-shareholding companies, a company with at least 51% Malaysian ownership may be exempted from the licence entirely — and from the RM1 million paid-up-capital requirement that comes with it. This is not guaranteed and comes down to the discretion of the officer handling the case, but it's a route worth raising with a client who is flexible on ownership structure.

### Application and documents

Applications are made on Form WRT 1, submitted to the Committee on Distributive Trade at KPDN — in practice through the BLESS online system. Only complete submissions are processed, and renewals must be filed at least three months before expiry. The checklist (last updated 18 July 2018) calls for:

- The completed WRT 1 form, plus a company profile / business plan and a detailed colour product catalogue or brochure.
- SSM records: latest ROC computer print-out (e-Info) and Form 49 / Section 14 (particulars of directors) certified by SSM; stamped Form 32A if shareholding has changed.
- LHDN tax registration confirmation (new company) or latest e-C acknowledgement (operating company).
- EPF registration proof (new) or the latest three months' contribution statement (operating).
- A valid local authority (PBT) business licence — or, if the company has none yet, a stamped tenancy agreement or sale-and-purchase agreement for the premises.
- Audited financial statements for the last three years, with a profit/loss justification.
- Any product approval letters where relevant: PBKD for medicines/health food, Suruhanjaya Tenaga or SIRIM for electrical/energy products, NPRA for cosmetics.
- Three sample transactions per month for the last three months: Customs documents (K1/K2), airway bill or bill of lading, DO/PO, and sales invoices.
- Foreign worker / expatriate details: names and valid employment pass or passport copies (with position, salary, nationality, job description), or an estimate of expatriates to be hired.
- For an exclusive restaurant: the menu and prices, plus the chef's academic certificate or proof of at least three years' culinary experience.
- Colour photographs of the premises, warehouse, office, and product samples.

### Why this matters for MISHU's immigration work

The expatriate section of the checklist is the tell: WRT/USS and Employment Pass applications are linked. A foreign-owned company generally needs its WRT or USS approval before KDN/Immigration will entertain Employment Passes for its expatriate staff, because that approval is what establishes the company is licensed to operate. When scoping a foreign-investor client who wants to bring in staff, check whether a WRT or USS licence is needed first — see [[fnb-composite-licence]] for the council-level premises licence that also feeds the document set. Note KPDN does not charge an application fee for either WRT or USS; the cost to the client is the professional service fee plus the RM1 million paid-up-capital requirement.
