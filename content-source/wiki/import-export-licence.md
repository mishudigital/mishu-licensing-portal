## Summary

Importing or exporting goods in Malaysia runs through the Royal Malaysian Customs Department (JKDM) under the Customs Act 1967. There is no single "import/export licence"; instead a trader makes customs declarations and — for controlled goods — obtains an Approved Permit (AP) or agency permit before the goods can move. The decisive scoping question is whether the specific goods are free, controlled, or prohibited: that is answered by the Customs (Prohibition of Imports/Exports) Orders, which sort goods into schedules. Most goods clear with a declaration and duty payment; controlled goods need a permit from the relevant Permit Issuing Agency (often MITI), applied for through the ePermit system; and some goods are outright prohibited. Food and live-animal products carry an extra layer of DVS, MAQIS, and MOH approvals. The whole system is online — Dagang Net for declarations, ePermit for permits, ePayment for duties.

## Body

### The framework

Import means bringing goods into Malaysia by land, sea, or air; export means taking them out. Either way the trader must declare to JKDM and pay any duty or tax due — import duty, excise duty, and sales tax on imports; export duty and cess on exports where applicable. The system is built around the Customs Act 1967 and a set of subsidiary orders (the Customs Duties Order for tariffs, the Customs Duties Exemption Order, and the Prohibition of Imports/Exports Orders for control).

### The general procedure (import, nine steps)

1. **Customs declaration.** All imports must be declared, by an appointed customs agent, by AEO self-declaration, or by a registered Direct User.
2. **Goods classification.** Get the correct tariff (HS) code to know what duty/tax applies — via the HS Explorer, the Customs Technical Services classification branch, the Customs Duties Order, or the relevant Free Trade Agreement.
3. **Check if the goods are controlled or prohibited.** Confirm against the Customs (Prohibition of Imports) Order 2017 whether the goods are restricted, and whether an import permit / approval is needed from an Other Government Agency (OGA) or Permit Issuing Agency (PIA). Any permit must be obtained *before* importing.
4. **Declaration and documents for clearance.** Declare using Customs Form No. 1 (K1) for imports — K2 for exports — electronically via Dagang Net eDeclare, and submit the import permit (via ePermit) where required. Supporting documents: bill of lading / airway bill, invoice, packing list, country-of-origin certificate (if relevant), and any others requested.
5. **Inspection.** JKDM may physically inspect consignments.
6. **Duty / tax payment.** Pay via counter or ePayment.
7. **Customs approval** — granted once duty/tax is paid and any permit obtained.
8. **Customs release.**
9. **Keep records for seven years** in Malaysia.

The export procedure mirrors this: declare to JKDM (K2), classify the goods, check the Prohibition of Exports Order for control, obtain any export permit, then clear.

### Controlled goods and the Approved Permit (AP)

The gating concept is the Prohibition Orders. Under the Customs Act 1967 and the Customs (Prohibition of Imports & Exports) Order, the import or export of certain goods is prohibited *except* where an import/export licence — an Approved Permit (AP) — has been granted, subject to the conditions stated in it. Goods fall into schedules: absolutely prohibited; prohibited unless an AP/licence is held; and so on. Whether a client needs an AP depends entirely on the specific goods and their HS code, so that classification is the first thing to settle.

APs are applied for and managed through **ePermit**, a web-based system run on Dagang Net linking traders with the Permit Issuing Agencies (PIA). MITI is a common AP issuer (e.g. for certain machinery and vehicles); other goods route to other agencies. The trader submits the permit application online, the issuing agency approves or rejects it, and the status updates in ePermit; the approved permit is then submitted with the customs declaration.

### Customs ruling (Ketetapan Kastam)

Where the correct tariff classification or duty treatment of goods is uncertain, a trader can apply to JKDM for an advance customs ruling using the Borang Permohonan Ketetapan Kastam. This gives certainty on classification before committing to import. The RAW folder holds the application form and a checklist for this.

### Food, meat, and live-animal products — the extra layer

Food and animal products carry approvals beyond Customs. The agencies involved are DVS (Department of Veterinary Services), MAQIS (Malaysian Quarantine and Inspection Services), and MOH, alongside Dagang Net. For meat specifically:

- The foreign abattoir / processing plant (loji pemprosesan) must already be recognised by Malaysia. Check the DVS list of recognised foreign abattoirs and processing plants for the source country; if the plant isn't recognised, it must first be registered through the e-Borang Veterinar process.
- Once the plant is recognised, register on e-Dagang and apply for the import permit.
- For export of meat, follow the destination country's requirements and apply for the Veterinary Health Certificate (Sijil Kesihatan Veterinar, SKV / VHC) through the DVS export section.

Halal considerations apply to meat — see [[halal-certificate]] for the JAKIM recognition that underpins which foreign plants are acceptable.

### MISHU service fee (worked example)

MISHU service fees vary widely by what the case actually needs, which reinforces the point that the goods and the regulatory path determine the work:

- **Import/export permit assessment (MITI)** — examining the prohibition order and advising on permit requirements for a given HS code (e.g. 8429.51, heavy machinery): RM530. Advice/report only, not the permit itself.
- **Import/export permit (KPDNHEP, via BLESS 2.0)** — paperwork, processing, and delivery of the permit (e.g. HS 847990): RM2,000.
- **Food/meat import permit (Dagang Net, DVS, MAQIS, MOH)** — e.g. luncheon meat from Korea and pet food from Thailand: RM8,000 inclusive, about five months.
- **IILS freight forwarding licence (MIDA)** — for a freight-forwarding business: RM10,000 (normal price RM12,000), roughly 4–6 months.

So a simple permit assessment is a few hundred ringgit of service, while a food-import or MIDA freight-forwarding case runs into five figures. Settle the goods, the HS code, and the regulatory route before quoting.
