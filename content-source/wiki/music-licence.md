## Summary

Any business that plays recorded music in public in Malaysia — background music in a cafe, a karaoke room, a hotel lobby, a gym, a live band in a bar — needs three separate music licences, not one. Each covers a different copyright held by a different group: MACP for the songwriters and composers, PPM for the record labels that own the sound recording, and RPM for the performers on that recording. All three are collective management organisations (CMOs) declared by MyIPO under the Copyright Act 1987. A premises typically needs a licence from all three to be fully covered. MISHU handles the paperwork, checking, and follow-up for all three bodies as one service line.

## Body

### Why three licences, not one

When a song plays in public, several distinct rights are in use at once, and Malaysian copyright law assigns each to a different rights-holder group represented by a different CMO:

- **MACP — Music Authors' Copyright Protection Berhad** (186403-X) covers the musical work itself: the composition and lyrics, owned by authors and composers. Office at Unit 8, Level U2, Block D4, Solaris Dutamas, Kuala Lumpur. Tel 03-6207 8638.
- **PPM — Public Performance Malaysia Berhad** (201601042369) covers the sound recording — the actual recorded track, owned and controlled by record companies (PPM members). A not-for-profit CMO declared by MyIPO under the Copyright Act 1987 (Act 332). Office at L-8-2, No. 2, Jalan Solaris, Solaris Mont' Kiara, Kuala Lumpur. Tel 03-6207 2888.
- **RPM — Recording Performers Malaysia Berhad** (201101036650 / 964784-U) covers the performers' right to equitable remuneration. This is a statutory right under Section 16B(1) of the Copyright Act 1987: users of commercial sound recordings must pay performers equitable remuneration for the public performance of those recordings. Office at Unit 15-09, Office Suites, One Mont Kiara, Kuala Lumpur. Tel 03-6411 8336.

A business that holds only one of the three is still exposed on the other two. For full coverage the team scopes all three together.

### Featured vs non-featured entertainment

This distinction runs through all three tariff schedules and drives which rate applies:

- **Featured entertainment** — music is the main feature: live bands, DJs, dancing, karaoke singing, product launches, entertainment by disc jockeys. Higher tariffs.
- **Non-featured entertainment** — music is background only: piped-in music, radio or TV broadcasts in a shop, lobby, or restaurant. Lower tariffs.

A premises offering non-featured entertainment that later runs a featured event (e.g. a restaurant hosting a live act) needs separate featured-entertainment cover for that event (PPM terms, clause 18).

### How the licence works (common to all three)

- **Validity:** 12 months from the commencement date (the day music usage started), renewed automatically unless cancelled in writing.
- **Supporting documents:** the application must be backed by the company's business registration (SSM) and/or the premises licence issued by the local council. MACP requires this explicitly; PPM requires supporting documents for verification and reserves the right to refuse incomplete applications.
- **Signatory:** an authorised person, director, or business owner of the company signs; multiple premises are listed on an attached schedule/appendix.
- **Fee basis:** calculated from the published standard tariff according to business category, capacity or floor area, and surcharges. PPM invoices are payable in full within 14 days; late payment carries 1% interest per month.
- **Right of entry:** the CMO may inspect the premises during business hours to verify the details the fee is based on.
- **Material changes:** the licence holder must notify the CMO within 14 days of any change of premises, address, or how music is used.

### Tariff structure — how fees are set

Each CMO publishes a standard tariff with the business categorised by type. Fees scale with capacity (persons or seats), floor area (sq ft), or room/unit count, plus per-item surcharges for TV monitors, video walls/multiple screens, and stand-alone karaoke units. Karaoke that reproduces digital copies attracts an additional reproduction tariff (PPM publishes a separate "Reproduction for Public Performance — Karaoke Recording" schedule).

Indicative figures for common F&B categories (rates change yearly — always check the current schedule):

- **PPM (effective 1 May 2026 – 30 Apr 2027), restaurant with pub or bar facility, non-featured:** RM1,520.70 for the first 50 persons, RM12.17 each additional; featured rates run far higher (a discotheque/nightclub/pub/bar at PP02 starts at RM4,055.20 for up to 50 persons). Eatery/coffee house/cafe/food court (PP04, non-featured): RM729.94 for the first 50 persons, RM3.04 each additional. Traditional coffee shop (PP05): RM304.14 first TV/audio player. Monitor surcharge RM202.76; video wall RM1,013.80; stand-alone karaoke RM1,013.80.
- **RPM (effective 1 October 2024), restaurant/cafe/coffee house/gaming centre (category 7):** featured RM650 first 50 seats + RM6.50 each additional; non-featured RM400 first 50 seats + RM4.00 each additional. Pub/bar/bistro/lounge (category 6): featured RM1,600 first 50 + RM16.00 each additional. Surcharges RM100/monitor, RM500/video wall, RM500/stand-alone karaoke.
- **RPM single-event rates** exist for one-off functions: wedding function RM200 flat, dinner & dance / product launch RM300 first 100 persons + RM1.50 each additional, exhibition/seminar/conference RM350 flat, concert RM500 first 1,000 persons (capped at RM5,000), blanket annual one-off licence RM20,000.

MACP publishes its own tariff schedule keyed to the same categories (its application form groups premises into the same business types — executive KTV/karaoke, pub/bar/bistro, restaurant/cafe/food court, retail/office/factory, and so on).

All tariffs are subject to yearly CPI adjustment and change without notice; the CMO decides the final categorisation of any establishment.
